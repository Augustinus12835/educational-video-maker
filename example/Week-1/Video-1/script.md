# Video 1: Risk vs Uncertainty - Understanding the Difference
**Duration:** 4 minutes (240 seconds)
**Target Word Count:** 600 words max (2.5 words/second)
**Course:** FIN101 Introduction to Financial Concepts

---

## Frame 0 (0:00-0:15) • 38 words

Here we'll look at risk versus uncertainty. These terms aren't interchangeable - they're fundamentally different. The left shows a probability distribution - that's risk. The right shows a black swan - that's uncertainty.

---

## Frame 1 (0:15-0:25) • 25 words

John Maynard Keynes, one of the most influential economists of the 20th century, had important insights about uncertainty.

---

## Frame 2 (0:25-0:40) • 38 words

Keynes said: "By uncertain knowledge, I do not mean merely to distinguish what is known for certain from what is only probable."

Uncertainty isn't just improbable - it's fundamentally unknowable.

---

## Frame 3 (0:40-0:58) • 45 words

Keynes continues: "The sense in which I use the term is that in which the prospect of a European war is uncertain, or the price of copper and the rate of interest twenty years hence are uncertain."

---

## Frame 4 (0:58-1:12) • 35 words

Warren Buffett adds: "Risk comes from not knowing what you are doing." Risk stems from ignorance or lack of understanding. These two perspectives define our framework.

---

## Frame 5 (1:12-1:30) • 45 words

Left side: Risk means known probability. Right side: Uncertainty means unknown probability.

Rolling a standard six-sided die demonstrates risk. Each outcome has one-in-six probability. We can measure it and assign probabilities.

---

## Frame 6 (1:30-1:45) • 38 words

Corporate bonds illustrate risk. Credit ratings, historical default rates, and financial statements provide data. We estimate default probabilities and model scenarios. Data makes it risk, not uncertainty.

---

## Frame 7 (1:45-2:10) • 63 words

Car insurance premiums demonstrate measurable risk. Actuarial tables allow insurance companies to assign probabilities to different accident types using driver age, past accidents, location, and vehicle type.

Your insurer analyzes your profile against millions of data points. They calculate precise probabilities for your demographic. Known probabilities mean measurable risk.

---

## Frame 8 (2:10-2:25) • 38 words

Now uncertainty. Technological disruption is the first example. Could anyone in 1995 predict smartphones would revolutionize banking? Could you assign probabilities? No - you couldn't imagine it coming.

---

## Frame 9 (2:25-2:45) • 50 words

COVID-19 demonstrates uncertainty perfectly. Early 2020, no one could model global lockdown probabilities or economic impacts. This was unprecedented. Without historical data, we can't assign meaningful probabilities. That's the core difference from risk.

---

## Frame 10 (2:45-3:00) • 38 words

Regime changes in stable countries represent uncertainty. A country stable for decades - can you calculate regime change probability? No. These are unique, one-off events without probability distributions.

---

## Frame 11 (3:00-4:00) • 150 words

Nassim Nicholas Taleb defined Black Swan events. A Black Swan has three attributes:

First, it's an outlier - outside regular expectations.

Second, it carries extreme impact.

Third, despite being an outlier, humans create explanations afterward, making it seem explainable and predictable.

After events happen, we convince ourselves we could've predicted them. But we couldn't. That's uncertainty.

So risk is measurable - we assign probabilities, model it, hedge with derivatives. Uncertainty involves unknown unknowns - we can't assign probabilities and need different strategies. This distinction is fundamental.
